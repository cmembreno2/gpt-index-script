What's a Generative Language Model?
A Generative Language Models, or ‚ÄúLM,‚Äù for short, is a piece of software takes as input a text prompt, like:

LMs can output single words (‚ÄúIt‚Äôs raining cats and dogs‚Äù), sentences (‚ÄúIt‚Äôs raining cats and dogs and I really wish I brought an umbrella‚Äù), or text of unbounded length.

How do these models work? How do they determine what text should come next?

You can think of language models as sophisticated pattern-matching machines. Given a very large training dataset (say, millions of public web documents), LMs learn patterns in language, from low-level grammatical rules to common expressions to high-level concepts, like how to produce fluid-sounding conversation.

Given a text prompt, an LM will try to complete the pattern, like:

To be or not to be
It was the best of times, it was the worst of times

For these common phrases, what it means to ‚Äúcomplete the pattern‚Äù is pretty clear. A language model that‚Äôs been trained on millions of web documents will have seen these sayings in its training data many times, and figuring out ‚Äúwhat comes next‚Äù is as straightforward as outputting the most common phrase.

But what about more complicated text prompts, like these?

Paris is to France as Tokyo is to Japan.
Pizza was invented in Naples, Italy.

Figuring out the ‚Äúright‚Äù answer here requires an understanding of basic math, analogies, and knowing that pizza was invented in Naples. Incredibly, modern language models are able to do all of these things, to varying degrees. They can grasp basic logic, answer simple math problems, and‚Äîthanks to their vast training datasets‚Äîare knowledgeable on a wide range of topics. Now, some of this is simple regurgitation; an LM might get a math problem right because it‚Äôs seen ‚Äú2+2=4‚Äù written many times on the internet. However, recent research suggests the most sophisticated language models can reason through simple word problems and even show their work.

Now, for each of the prompts above, it was obvious what text should come next. If we said pizza was invented in Sante Fe, we‚Äôd just be plain wrong. But what about fill-in-the-blank in cases where there‚Äôs no ‚Äúright answer?‚Äù Like the prompt:

Which brings us to one of the most fascinating qualities of generative language models: they can supply us with a whole array of possible answers, not just one, like:

In this way, generative language models are like the ultimate improviser: given any text prompt, they can produce dozens of plausible responses on the fly that aren‚Äôt prewritten. This versatility means that a single model can power a near-infinite number of language applications.

However, it's exactly this versatility that makes it tricky to build applications with LMs. A model that can say anything can say anything. LaMDA: A Language Model for Dialog Applications
Under the hood, the AI Test Kitchen experiments are powered by a language model called LaMDA. (To see LaMDA in action, check out our demo at the Google I/O keynote.) It was fine-tuned to produce text that‚Äôs not only realistic, but also sensible, specific, and interesting, and which is less likely to be biased or offensive than the output of vanilla language models. So, you can give it one turn of a conversation, like this:

And LaMDA, drawing on its vast dialogue training data set, might generate a reply like:

This is a perfectly reasonable reply, but as before, there are many potentially good ways LaMDA could have responded to this question. Like the language models we talked about earlier, LaMDA can also provide us with several alternative responses:

LaMDA as a storytelling partner
In our Imagine It demo, we wanted to see if we could transform LaMDA into a storytelling partner. If we gave it an imaginary place, could LaMDA help us imagine it in vivid detail?

Response: This underwater jazz performance is so epic! There are thousands of sea creatures all around jamming. A sting ray is carrying the melody on a trombone. And whoa, that sea turtle is really rocking out on the drums! You feel the rhythm of jazz is your bones!

What we're trying to do here is establish a particular tone (whimsical, imaginative, zany) and format (three or four sentences describing how things look and feel, as if we were there) for our language app. By prompting LaMDA with a couple of examples like this, we can teach it to generate responses in the same style. LaMDA staying on-topic
In Talk About It (Dog Edition), we wanted users to be able to have open-ended conversations with LaMDA, but for those conversations to stay on the topic of dogs. Though this is an ongoing area of research, in our Talk About It demo, we tried to solve this topicality problem with a simple approach: priming. In other words, we gave LaMDA a bunch of examples of pivoting a conversation to the topic of dogs, like this:

Response: Well, I don't really keep up with human music. But I could tell you why I think it'd be so fun if dogs formed bands!! They have amazing senses of hearing and I love listening to them howl.

Once we primed LaMDA with a few examples like these, it was able to turn the conversation back to dogs all by itself, even when we tried to change the subject:

Note that this doesn‚Äôt always work perfectly, but it does suggest one type of approach we might use to build conversational apps that stay on topic.
